The pass, in two steps

A new sample gets characterized in two stages. Steady state first — it is cheap, it carries no time-resolution artifacts to misread, and it tells you where to point everything that follows. Then time-resolved: emission dynamics, spatial transport, and pump–probe.

Step 1 — Linear / steady-state spectrum Read the Andor .asc and calibrate the spatial axis (real or k-space); decide whether you even need k-space; linear reflectance (RC, backgrounds, lineshapes, the stack); photoluminescence; parameter sweeps.
Step 2 — Time-resolved TRPL time traces (TCSPC, IRF-convolved fits); spatial-profile evolution by point-scan or SPAD array; MSD & transport regimes; multi-species imaging; and transient reflectance / TA maps.

Zero-th rule: every routine here is checked on synthetic data with known ground truth before it touches a measurement. If it cannot recover what was put in, it does not get used. Deferred for now: the k-space toolkit for strongly dispersive samples (polariton dispersion fitting etc.) — its own step, later.

Python — parse the .asc, real-space & k-space calibration 
def read_asc(text):
    rows = []
    for line in text.splitlines():
        line = line.strip()
        if not line or line.startswith("#"):        # skip comment header
            continue
        rows.append([float(p) for p in line.replace(",", "\t").split()])
    arr = np.asarray(rows)
    return arr[:, 0], arr[:, 1:]        # wavelength_nm, (n_wl, n_px) image

def um_per_px(pitch_um=16.0, f_obj_mm=2.0, f_tube_mm=200.0):
    return pitch_um / (f_tube_mm / f_obj_mm)          # M = f_tube / f_obj

def k_space_angle_axis(n_px, edge_lo, edge_hi, NA):
    """BFP disk edge sits at the objective NA; position is linear in sin(theta)."""
    center, radius = 0.5*(edge_lo+edge_hi), 0.5*(edge_hi-edge_lo)
    s = np.clip(NA*(np.arange(n_px)-center)/radius, -0.999, 0.999)
    return np.degrees(np.arcsin(s))                   # angle axis stretches near the edge

1.2Do you need k-space?

Only if the feature disperses inside the light cone your objective collects. Dispersion is set by the mode's effective mass: \(E(\theta)=E_0+\hbar^2 k_\parallel^2/2m^*\) with \(k_\parallel=(E_0/\hbar c)\sin\theta\). A very light mode (cavity photon, polariton) bends steeply and demands k-space imaging — a single-angle spectrum samples one arbitrary point of a curve you cannot reconstruct. A heavy mode (a bare emitter, \(m^*\!\sim\! m_0\)) is flat, and real-space imaging tells you more. Slide \(m^*\) across six decades:

1.3aLinear reflectance: RC & the background problem

reflectance contrast
$$ \mathrm{RC}(E)=\frac{R_\text{sample}(E)-R_\text{ref}(E)}{R_\text{ref}(E)} $$

RC divides out the lamp spectrum and system response — if you have a reference. With a bare-substrate region you measure it (subfigure 2, blue). Without one — a planar cavity is everywhere — you must estimate it: mask the feature and interpolate the background across the feature-free wavelengths to synthesize a reference (red). The feature here sits deliberately on the sloped edge of the white light, the hard case.

Open question: the best reference-free background. Interpolation is a decent approximation, not exact — the recovered depth and lineshape drift with the interpolation window and order. Better ideas welcome.

Python — reference-free background by masked interpolation 
# mask the feature, fit a low-order polynomial through the rest -> synthetic reference
def estimate_background(E, R, feature_window=(715, 735), order=2):
    m = (E < feature_window[0]) | (E > feature_window[1])
    return np.polyval(np.polyfit(E[m], R[m], order), E)

rc_measured  = (R_sample - R_substrate)              / R_substrate
rc_reference_free = (R_sample - estimate_background(E, R_sample)) \
                    / estimate_background(E, R_sample)

1.3bLineshapes: Lorentzian, Gaussian, Fano (+ phase)

Fano, with a constant background phase φ (reduces to the classic form at φ = 0)
$$ F(\varepsilon)=\frac{\big(q+\varepsilon\cos\varphi-\sin\varphi\big)^2+\big(\cos\varphi-1+\varepsilon\sin\varphi\big)^2}{1+\varepsilon^2}, \qquad \varepsilon=\frac{2(E-E_0)}{\Gamma} $$
  • Lorentzian — one damped resonance; heavy \(1/\Delta E^2\) tails.
  • Gaussian — inhomogeneous / resolution broadening; tails die fast.
  • Fano — a discrete resonance interfering with a continuum. \(q\!\to\!\infty\) recovers a Lorentzian peak, \(q=0\) a symmetric dip; a background phase \(\varphi\) tilts it further. The apparent extremum is offset from \(E_0\) by \(\sim\Gamma/2q\) — reading a peak position off an asymmetric line is wrong.
  • p0 / bounds: center from the extremum (bounded to the window); width from the half-max span (bounded below by the instrument resolution); amplitude from extremum − baseline. A parameter that pins to a bound means the wrong model or window.

1.3cWhy RC looks Fano: the stack matters

the stack that turns a symmetric oscillator into an asymmetric RC

Put a perfectly symmetric Lorentz oscillator in a thin layer and compute the RC with a transfer matrix (TMM): the lineshape comes out asymmetric, and its sign and shape swing with the oxide thickness — pure interference between the resonant reflection and the rest of the stack. So before reading physics into an inverted or dispersive RC, check the stack.

1.4Photoluminescence

Same detector, same .asc, same peak-fitting recipe as reflectance (§1.3b). But two things are specific to emission. Peaks are fit exactly as in reflectance — what changes is how to read the intensity.

(1) PL is time-integrated. A camera counts every photon per pulse, so the recorded intensity of a peak is \(\propto I_0\,\tau\), not \(I_0\). Two peaks with instantaneous-intensity ratio \(a\) but lifetime ratio \(b\) show an integrated ratio \(a\,b\). So a peak can look bright merely by being long-lived, and "same average power" changes meaning with rep rate. (Here lifetimes ≪ rep period, single-exponential, no rise.)

intensity ratio a (peak1/peak2)
lifetime ratio b (τ1/τ2)

(2) The spatial profile is not the laser. Whatever is excited moves before it emits; for diffusion during an exponential lifetime the variances add, \(\sigma_\text{PL}^2=\sigma_\text{laser}^2+2D\tau\), giving a diffusion length from two second moments (a Gaussian fit is core-weighted and biased low once the time-integrated profile grows heavy wings).

$$ \sigma_\text{PL}^2=\sigma_\text{laser}^2+2D\tau \;\Rightarrow\; L_D=\sqrt{D\tau}=\sqrt{\tfrac{\sigma_\text{PL}^2-\sigma_\text{laser}^2}{2}} $$

2.1TRPL: the time trace (TCSPC)

how a PicoHarp builds the histogram — time-correlated single-photon counting

Each detected photon's arrival relative to the laser sync fills one bin. Bin width = 4 ps × 2^binning; acquire for tacq_ms; set CFD level / zero-cross, sync divider, offsets. Saved CSV: time_ps, counts.

what you fit — multi-exponential decay convolved with a Gaussian IRF (Origin "ConvolutedDecay4n")
$$ I(t)=y_0+\sum_{i=1}^{4} A_i\, \exp\!\Big(\tfrac{w^2-2\tau_i(t-t_0)}{2\tau_i^{2}}\Big)\, \mathrm{erfc}\!\Big(\tfrac{w^2-\tau_i(t-t_0)}{\sqrt{2}\,w\,\tau_i}\Big) \;+\; y_s\,\mathrm{erfc}\!\Big(\tfrac{-(t-t_0)}{\sqrt{2}\,w}\Big) $$

Parameters: y0 dark floor, t0 pulse arrival, amplitudes A,B,C,D with lifetimes t1..t4, IRF width w (Gaussian σ), optional step ys. Never deconvolve (it amplifies noise) — fit this analytic convolution, weighted by \(\sqrt{\text{counts}}\), and cross-check with the model-free \(1/e\) time. 2.2Spatial profile evolution: point-scan vs SPAD array

Two routes to \(\sigma(t)\). Point scan: a single-pixel detector behind the pinhole; two stages raster x,y to image a plane, one full decay per point — complete time resolution, but slow (N² acquisitions) and drift-sensitive. SPAD array: 23 pixels record at once; they don't image the spot, they sample it, and a 2D-Gaussian fit per time bin gives σₓ, σ_y.

the 23-pixel SPAD layout — rows of 5-4-5-4-5, pixel 11 at center

Pitch 23 µm (x) × 19.92 µm (y); coordinates in µm feed the fit directly.

per-time-bin 2D Gaussian fit → σ(t)
$$ I(x,y)=A\,e^{-\frac{(x-x_0)^2}{2\sigma_x^2}-\frac{(y-y_0)^2}{2\sigma_y^2}}+\text{off}, \quad \sigma_\text{eq}=\sqrt{\tfrac{\sigma_x^2+\sigma_y^2}{2}} $$

p0: A=max−min, (x0,y0)=centroid, σx=σy=20 µm, off=min. Fit each frame; seed frame \(k\!+\!1\) from \(k\) with bounds tightened to ±18% (the same continuity trick as §1.5). The reported width is \(\sigma_\text{eq}\); the transport plot uses \(\sigma_\text{eq}^2\) vs time.

2.3Interpreting σ(t): MSD & transport regimes

Plot \(\mathrm{MSD}(t)=\sigma^2(t)-\sigma^2(0)\) and read the physics off the shape — carefully. Four mechanisms broaden a profile; only two are motion. Diffusion (MSD = 2Dt, linear), ballistic (v²t², superlinear), annihilation (bimolecular decay eats the dense center first, σ grows with zero transport), emission saturation (the center clips early, apparent σ starts high and shrinks). Pick one and scrub time. Acceptance test before claiming a D: MSD linear over a decade and D independent of pump power (a power-dependent "D" is the annihilation tell).

2.5Transient reflectance (TA): the files

each scan writes two files — a "para" header and a "sum" data blob

sum.txt is tab-delimited (--/---→0). Frame \(i\) occupies rows \([i\cdot\text{crop\_w}:(i{+}1)\cdot\text{crop\_w}]\), read as (crop_w, crop_h) then np.rot90 → (crop_h, crop_w). Axes end up (delay ps, position µm, spectral px→nm). Signal is ΔR/R (or ΔT/T).

2.5aWhat makes a dR/R signal (not always population)

The pump changes the pump-off resonance three ways, each with its own fingerprint on \(\Delta R/R_0=(R_\text{on}-R_\text{off})/R_\text{off}\): amplitude (bleach/induced-absorption) → one lobe like the resonance itself, the population-like term; shift → a dispersive two-lobe (first-derivative); broadening → three-lobe (second-derivative). Shift and broadening give large signals with ~zero population change. Toggle the pump-off lineshape between a Lorentzian dip and a Fano, and mix the three effects:
