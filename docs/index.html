The pass, in two steps

A new sample gets characterized in two stages. Steady state first — it is cheap, it carries no time-resolution artifacts to misread, and it tells you where to point everything that follows. Then time-resolved: emission dynamics, spatial transport, and pump–probe.

Step 1 — Linear / steady-state spectrum Read the Andor .asc and calibrate the spatial axis (real or k-space); decide whether you need k-space; linear reflectance (RC, backgrounds, lineshapes, the stack); photoluminescence; parameter sweeps.
Step 2 — Time-resolved TRPL time traces (TCSPC, IRF-convolved fits); spatial-profile evolution by point-scan or SPAD array; MSD & transport regimes; multi-species imaging; transient reflectance / TA maps.

Deferred for now: the k-space toolkit for strongly dispersive samples (polariton dispersion fitting etc.) — its own step, later. Everything here runs on synthetic data with known ground truth.

Python — parse the .asc, real-space & k-space calibration 
def read_asc(text):
    rows = [ [float(p) for p in ln.replace(",","\t").split()]
             for ln in text.splitlines()
             if ln.strip() and not ln.startswith("#") ]
    a = np.asarray(rows)
    return a[:,0], a[:,1:]                        # wavelength_nm, (n_wl, n_px)

def um_per_px(pitch_um=16.0, f_obj_mm=2.0, f_tube_mm=200.0):
    return pitch_um / (f_tube_mm / f_obj_mm)      # M = f_tube / f_obj

def k_space_angle_axis(n_px, lo, hi, NA):         # disk edge = NA; linear in sin(theta)
    c, r = 0.5*(lo+hi), 0.5*(hi-lo)
    return np.degrees(np.arcsin(np.clip(NA*(np.arange(n_px)-c)/r, -.999, .999)))

1.2Do you need k-space?

Only if the feature disperses inside the light cone your objective collects: \(E(\theta)=E_0+\hbar^2 k_\parallel^2/2m^*\), \(k_\parallel=(E_0/\hbar c)\sin\theta\). A very light mode (cavity photon, polariton) bends steeply and demands k-space; a heavy mode (\(m^*\!\sim\! m_0\)) is flat, and real-space imaging tells you more. Slide \(m^*\) across six decades:

1.3aLinear reflectance: RC & the background problem

reflectance contrast
$$ \mathrm{RC}(E)=\frac{R_\text{sample}(E)-R_\text{ref}(E)}{R_\text{ref}(E)} $$

RC divides out the lamp spectrum and system response — if you have a reference. With a bare-substrate region you measure it (blue). Without one, mask the feature and interpolate the feature-free wavelengths to synthesize a reference (red). The feature here sits deliberately on the sloped edge of the white light, the hard case.

Open question: the best reference-free background. Interpolation is a decent approximation, not exact — the recovered depth and lineshape drift with window and order.

1.3bLineshapes: Lorentzian, Gaussian, Fano (+ phase)

Fano with a constant background phase φ (reduces to the classic form at φ = 0)
$$ F(\varepsilon)=\frac{(q+\varepsilon\cos\varphi-\sin\varphi)^2+(\cos\varphi-1+\varepsilon\sin\varphi)^2}{1+\varepsilon^2}, \qquad \varepsilon=\frac{2(E-E_0)}{\Gamma} $$
  • Lorentzian — one damped resonance; heavy \(1/\Delta E^2\) tails.
  • Gaussian — inhomogeneous / resolution broadening; tails die fast.
  • Fano — discrete resonance interfering with a continuum. \(q\!\to\!\infty\) → Lorentzian peak, \(q=0\) → symmetric dip; a phase \(\varphi\) tilts it. The extremum is offset from \(E_0\) by \(\sim\Gamma/2q\).
  • p0 / bounds: center from the extremum (bounded to the window); width from the half-max span; amplitude from extremum − baseline. A pinned parameter means the wrong model or window.

1.3cWhy RC looks Fano: the stack matters

the stack that turns a symmetric oscillator into an asymmetric RC

Put a perfectly symmetric Lorentz oscillator in a thin layer and compute the RC with a transfer matrix: the lineshape comes out asymmetric, and its sign and shape swing with the oxide thickness — pure interference with the rest of the stack. Check the stack before reading physics into an inverted or dispersive RC.

1.4Photoluminescence

Same detector, same .asc, same peak-fitting recipe as reflectance (§1.3b). Two things are specific to emission.

(1) PL is time-integrated. A camera counts every photon per pulse, so a peak's recorded intensity is \(\propto I_0\tau\), not \(I_0\). Two peaks with instantaneous ratio \(a\) but lifetime ratio \(b\) show an integrated ratio \(ab\) — a peak can look bright just by being long-lived, and "same average power" changes meaning with rep rate. (Lifetimes ≪ rep period, single-exponential, no rise.)

intensity ratio a
lifetime ratio b

(2) The spatial profile is not the laser. Whatever is excited moves before it emits; for diffusion during an exponential lifetime the variances add, giving a diffusion length from two second moments (a Gaussian fit is core-weighted and biased low once the profile grows heavy wings).

$$ \sigma_\text{PL}^2=\sigma_\text{laser}^2+2D\tau \;\Rightarrow\; L_D=\sqrt{D\tau}=\sqrt{\tfrac{\sigma_\text{PL}^2-\sigma_\text{laser}^2}{2}} $$

1.5Sweeps: tracking a peak vs power / temperature / gate

The data becomes a stack of spectra vs a control knob. To track a species I fit sequentially: step \(k\)'s result seeds step \(k\!+\!1\), with bounds tightened to a window around it. Identity is preserved by continuity — it will smooth over a genuine abrupt transition, so watch the residuals. Independent per-step fits swap identities the moment two peaks overlap. 2.1TRPL: the time trace (TCSPC)

how a PicoHarp builds the histogram — time-correlated single-photon counting

Each photon's arrival relative to the laser sync fills one bin. Bin width = 4 ps × 2^binning; acquire for tacq_ms; set CFD level / zero-cross, sync divider, offsets. Saved CSV: time_ps, counts.

what you fit — multi-exponential decay convolved with a Gaussian IRF (Origin "ConvolutedDecay4n")
$$ I(t)=y_0+\sum_{i=1}^{4} A_i\,\exp\!\Big(\tfrac{w^2-2\tau_i(t-t_0)}{2\tau_i^{2}}\Big)\, \mathrm{erfc}\!\Big(\tfrac{w^2-\tau_i(t-t_0)}{\sqrt{2}\,w\,\tau_i}\Big) + y_s\,\mathrm{erfc}\!\Big(\tfrac{-(t-t_0)}{\sqrt{2}\,w}\Big) $$

Parameters: y0 dark floor, t0 pulse arrival, amplitudes A,B,C,D with lifetimes t1..t4, IRF width w (Gaussian σ), optional step ys. Never deconvolve — fit this analytic convolution, weighted by \(\sqrt{\text{counts}}\), and cross-check with the model-free \(1/e\) time.

2.2Spatial profile evolution: point-scan vs SPAD array

Two routes to \(\sigma(t)\). Point scan: a single-pixel detector; two stages raster x,y to image a plane, one full decay per point — complete time resolution, but slow (N²) and drift-sensitive. SPAD array: 23 pixels record at once; they don't image the spot, they sample it, and a 2D-Gaussian fit per time bin gives σₓ, σ_y.

the 23-pixel SPAD layout — rows of 5-4-5-4-5, pixel 11 at center

Pitch 23 µm (x) × 19.92 µm (y); pixel coordinates in µm feed the fit directly.

per-time-bin 2D Gaussian fit → σ(t)
$$ I(x,y)=A\,\exp\!\Big(-\tfrac{(x-x_0)^2}{2\sigma_x^2}-\tfrac{(y-y_0)^2}{2\sigma_y^2}\Big)+\text{offset}, \qquad \sigma_\text{eq}=\sqrt{\tfrac{\sigma_x^2+\sigma_y^2}{2}} $$

p0: A = max−min, (x0,y0) = centroid, σx = σy = 20 µm, off = min. Fit each frame; seed frame \(k\!+\!1\) from \(k\) with bounds tightened to ±18% (same continuity trick as §1.5). The transport plot uses \(\sigma_\text{eq}^2\) vs time.

2.3Interpreting σ(t): MSD & transport regimes

Plot \(\mathrm{MSD}(t)=\sigma^2(t)-\sigma^2(0)\) and read the physics off the shape — carefully. Diffusion (2Dt, linear), ballistic (v²t²), annihilation (eats the dense center first, σ grows with zero transport), emission saturation (center clips early, σ starts high and shrinks). Acceptance test before claiming a D: MSD linear over a decade and D independent of pump power.

2.5Transient reflectance (TA): the files

each scan writes two files — a "para" header and a "sum" data blob

sum.txt is tab-delimited (--/---→0). Frame \(i\) occupies rows \([i\cdot\text{crop\_w}:(i{+}1)\cdot\text{crop\_w}]\), read as (crop_w, crop_h) then np.rot90 → (crop_h, crop_w). Axes: (delay ps, position µm, spectral px→nm). Signal is ΔR/R.

2.5aWhat makes a dR/R signal (not always population)

The pump changes the pump-off resonance three ways, each with its own fingerprint on \(\Delta R/R_0\): amplitude → one lobe like the resonance (population-like); shift → dispersive two-lobe (first-derivative); broadening → three-lobe (second-derivative). Shift and broadening give large signals with ~zero population change. Toggle Lorentzian dip ↔ Fano and mix:
